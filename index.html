Principal Investigator

Dr. Satish Kumar

Dr. Kumar is the Rae S. and Frank H. Neely Professor in the George W. Woodruff School of Mechanical Engineering at Georgia Tech. His research centers on thermal management, multi-physics transport models, application of machine learning techniques to develop high-fidelity multi-scale models, and high-precision thermal metrology to analyze thermal materials.

His work proceeds systematically from new insights into thermal transport principles at the micro-nano scale to the development of innovative transport models and experimental demonstrations at the mesoscale and continuum scale. Dr. Kumar is an ASME Fellow and author of over 160 refereed journal and conference publications.

What We Do

Research Highlights

🔬

Nanoscale Thermal Transport

Atomistic and first-principles modeling of electro-thermal transport in 1D/2D nanostructures, interfaces, and wide-bandgap materials (GaN, Ga₂O₃, hBN, graphene).

⚡

Power Electronics Cooling

Design and analysis of thermal management solutions for high-power-density electronics including GaN HEMTs, SiC devices, and embedded thermoelectrics.

🤖

Machine Learning for Thermal Analysis

ML-enabled multi-scale models for rapid and accurate thermal transport analysis of 3D electronic packages, electric motors, and data center systems.

📡

Thermal Metrology

Ultra-fast thermoreflectance imaging and frequency-domain thermoreflectance (FDTR) techniques for high-fidelity characterization of electronic devices and materials.

🏭

Systems-Level Thermal Management

Meso-scale modeling and experimental validation for thermal reliability of data centers, electric motors, and forward operating base systems.

🧪

2D Materials & Interfaces

Fundamental transport mechanisms in boron nitride, graphene, nanotubes, and polymer composites promising for next-generation micro- and power-electronics.

Prior to that, he worked at IBM Corporation, where he was responsible for the thermal management of electronic devices.

His research centers on thermal management, multi-physics transport models, application of machine learning techniques to develop high-fidelity multi-scale models, and high-precision thermal metrology to analyze thermal materials. His research proceeds systematically from new insights into thermal transport principles at the micro-nano scale to the development of innovative transport models and experimental demonstrations at the mesoscale and continuum scale.

He is author or co-author of over 160 refereed journal and conference publications and is an ASME Fellow and Woodruff Faculty Fellow.

What We Study

Research Overview

The MiNDS lab establishes multi-physics and multi-scale computational and experimental frameworks to enhance the energy efficiency, performance, and reliability of electronic devices and systems. Our work spans from atomistic simulations to system-level thermal management.

Topic 01

Thermal Management of Electronic Devices & Electric Machines

We focus on thermal management across different length scales, from chip-level thermo-electrics and heat spreaders to advanced two-phase cooling for compact electric vehicle (EV) motors. By developing coupled electro-magnetics and CFD-HT models, we address the limitations of conventional single-phase cooling to enable higher power and torque densities.

[ Add Figure for Topic 01 ]
Topic 02

Multi-scale Transport Modeling of Nanoscale Materials

As devices scale down, Fourier's law fails. We explore thermal transport physics across novel materials and interfaces using atomistic simulations (DFT, AGF, MD). We also integrate machine learning (ML) to develop ML-based interatomic potentials for fast, accurate simulations and to study phenomena like defect influence and phonon transmission.

[ Add Figure for Topic 02 ]
Topic 03

Ultra-Wide Bandgap Materials & Devices

We investigate electro-thermal transport in emerging ultra-wide bandgap materials like β-Ga₂O₃ for power and RF electronics. Using a combination of advanced modeling and thermal metrology techniques, we tackle self-heating challenges to enable higher breakdown voltages and switching efficiencies in field effect transistors (FETs).

[ Add Figure for Topic 03 ]
Topic 04

Flexible Electronics

We leverage additive technologies like aerosol jet printing (AJP) and inkjet printing to create low-cost electronic components and sensor platforms. By optimizing inks containing silver nanoparticles, carbon nanotubes (CNTs), and hexagonal boron nitride, we fabricate and optimize devices such as high-performance CNT FETs.

Lab Infrastructure

Facilities & Equipment

The MiNDS lab houses state-of-the-art experimental and computational resources for thermal transport research.

[ Image: FDTR System ]

Frequency-Domain Thermoreflectance (FDTR) System

Our custom-built FDTR system enables measurement of thermal conductivity, thermal boundary resistance, and heat capacity of thin films and bulk materials at the nanoscale. It serves as a cornerstone for high-fidelity thermal metrology of electronic devices and materials.

[ Image: Ultra-Fast System ]

Ultra-Fast Thermoreflectance Imaging

High-speed thermoreflectance imaging for mapping thermal distributions across active electronic devices, providing critical insights into heat dissipation pathways in novel semiconductor topologies.

[ Image: HPC Resources ]

High-Performance Computing Cluster

Dedicated HPC resources for molecular dynamics, DFT, Monte Carlo, and ML-based thermal simulations, supporting our multi-physics transport models from the atomistic to the continuum scales.
