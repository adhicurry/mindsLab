Principal Investigator

Dr. Satish Kumar

Dr. Kumar is the Rae S. and Frank H. Neely Professor in the George W. Woodruff School of Mechanical Engineering at Georgia Tech. His research centers on thermal management, multi-physics transport models, application of machine learning techniques to develop high-fidelity multi-scale models, and high-precision thermal metrology to analyze thermal materials.

His work proceeds systematically from new insights into thermal transport principles at the micro-nano scale to the development of innovative transport models and experimental demonstrations at the mesoscale and continuum scale. Dr. Kumar is an ASME Fellow and author of over 160 refereed journal and conference publications.

What We Study

Research Overview

The MiNDS lab establishes multi-physics and multi-scale computational and experimental frameworks to enhance the energy efficiency, performance, and reliability of electronic devices and systems. Our work spans from atomistic simulations to system-level thermal management.

Topic 01

Thermal Management of Electronic Devices & Electric Machines

We focus on thermal management across different length scales — from chip-level thermoelectric coolers and 2D material heat spreaders to advanced two-phase cooling for compact EV and HEV motors. We develop coupled electro-magnetic and CFD-HT models and propose novel latent heat-driven evaporative cooling that delivers up to 3× higher power and torque density compared to conventional single-phase jacket cooling.

Topic 01

Thermal Management of Electronic
Devices & Electric Machines

Multi-scale thermal management from chip-level hot spots to electric vehicle motors — combining coupled electro-magnetic modeling, two-phase cooling, thermoelectrics, and 2D material heat spreaders.

Motor Thermal Mgmt Thermoelectric Coolers 2D Heat Spreaders Publications
🔩
Sub-topic A

Motor Thermal Management

Permanent Magnet Synchronous Motors (PMSMs) are central to electric vehicle (EV) and hybrid electric vehicle (HEV) drivetrains because of their high power factor and torque density. However, high heat generation as a consequence of copper loss and stator/rotor core losses frequently reduces the efficiency and longevity of the electric motor. Finding an appropriate thermal management system to maintain motor temperature below operational limits while enhancing power density is a major challenge for thermal designers.

To develop appropriate heat rejection techniques, we first accurately quantify component-wise electro-magnetic heat losses and peak temperatures using a two-way iteratively coupled electro-magnetic and thermal modeling framework. This allows us to precisely predict efficiency, component-wise heat losses, and corresponding temperature distributions.

Building on this foundation, we propose a novel concept of latent heat-driven, evaporation-assisted two-phase cooling targeting motor hot spots. The state-of-the-art single-phase liquid cooling becomes inadequate for compact motors due to irregular geometry and non-uniform heat generation. Our evaporative cooling approach removes significantly more heat than single-phase liquid cooling.

Key Result: Numerical fully-coupled electro-thermal analysis demonstrates evaporative cooling enables 3× higher power and torque density in a significantly smaller motor volume compared to conventional jacket-cooled designs, validated on a 125 kW BMW i3 motor topology.

For real-time temperature monitoring without expensive CFD/HT simulation, we developed a highly discretized 3D Lumped Parameter Thermal Network (LPTN) model. The LPTN successfully predicts electro-thermal performance over custom drive cycles and can be paired with optimization algorithms and machine learning techniques to further refine the evaporative cooling system design.

Methods & Tools

⚡
Sub-topic B

Thermoelectric Coolers for Microelectronics

The exploration of ultrathin thermoelectric coolers (TECs) on the active side of microelectronic devices represents another major thread of our research. As power densities rise and hot spots become increasingly localized, conventional heat sinking solutions fail to remove heat efficiently from specific small-area regions.

Our research analyzes the efficient use of ultrathin TECs by focusing on critical practical issues including:

  • Integration of TEC devices within the electronic package stack
  • Effect of parasitic thermal and electrical contact resistances on cooling performance
  • Utilization of appropriate current pulses and feedback control algorithms to maximize transient cooling efficiency
  • Coupled operation of multiple TEC arrays for cooling spatiotemporally varying hot spots, maintaining dynamic hot-spot temperature below threshold limits
Approach: We combine 3D compact electro-thermal models of packaged TEC stacks with experimental validation, enabling design-space exploration across packaging configurations, current waveforms, and multi-TEC array topologies.
📱
Sub-topic C

2D Material Heat Spreaders for Portable Electronics

The constant miniaturization of electronic components and the increasing use of mobile devices has pushed the limits of current thermal solutions. 2D materials with high in-plane thermal conductivity — such as graphene and hexagonal boron nitride (h-BN) — have significant potential for hot spot management in portable electronics.

We focus on using these 2D materials as ultra-thin heat spreaders to laterally redistribute heat from hot spots to cooler package regions. A key challenge is transferring these materials from their growth substrate to arbitrary device substrates without degrading their thermal properties or disrupting existing fabrication processes.

  • Developed a process to transfer h-BN from its growth substrate to arbitrary substrates (e.g., silicon chips) while preserving thermal conductivity
  • Transfer process compatible with industrial fabrication workflows
  • Impact of different transfer processes on h-BN thermal conductivity characterized via ultrafast thermal imaging
  • Benchmark of h-BN heat spreader performance against current commercial solutions

Topic 02

Multi-scale Transport Modeling of
Nanoscale Materials & Their Interfaces

First-principles and machine learning-based atomistic simulations to understand and predict thermal transport in novel electronic materials — from phonon transmission at 2D material interfaces to ML-accelerated interatomic potentials.

ML Interatomic Potentials Interface Phonon Transport Material Thermal Properties Publications
🤖
Sub-topic A

Machine Learning-Based Interatomic Potentials

The high computational demand of studying thermal transport physics using first-principles methods severely limits the number of unique systems and types of physical phenomena we can study. Machine learning (ML)-based interatomic potentials have recently emerged as a promising tool for large-scale atomic simulations due to their high accuracy at a fraction of the computational cost of quantum mechanical methods.

We develop ML-based interatomic potentials using our Voxelized Atomic Structure (VASt) framework — a convolutional neural network (CNN) approach that voxelizes atomic structures to generate feature representations for force prediction. The VASt framework predicts atomic forces with accuracy comparable to DFT at dramatically reduced cost, enabling long-timescale and large-system MD simulations.

  • VASt potentials for silicon: prediction of thermal conductivity as a function of isotropic strain, validated against DFT data
  • Study of complex thermal transport physics such as the effect of strain and defects on thermal conductivity in β-Ga₂O₃ and MoSe₂
  • Enables large-scale MD simulations of phenomena that are computationally prohibitive with pure first-principles methods
  • Extension to multi-component systems and interfaces for materials discovery
Highlight: The VASt framework achieves quantum mechanical accuracy using convolutional neural networks, enabling fast and reliable prediction of atomic forces and thermal properties across a wide range of strain and defect configurations.
Convolutional Neural Networks VASt Framework Molecular Dynamics DFT Validation
🔬
Sub-topic B

Phonon Transport at Material Interfaces

Thermal transport at the interfaces of low-dimensional nanostructures such as two-dimensional (2D) MoS₂ and graphene is critically important for nanoscale electronic devices. Phonons are expected to be the dominant energy carriers for interfacial thermal transport, and a fundamental understanding of phonon transport in these nanostructures and across their interfaces is needed for efficient thermal management.

We use first-principles density functional theory (DFT) and the atomistic Green's function (AGF) method to study phonon transmission and thermal boundary conductance (TBC) at material interfaces. Recent work has investigated:

  • Relationship between interfacial electronic structure and phonon properties at monolayer MoS₂/metal substrate interfaces (Au vs. Sc — physisorbed vs. chemisorbed)
  • Effects of different lattice stacking configurations on phonon transmission and TBC at h-BN/single-layer graphene/h-BN heterostructures
  • Temperature-dependent TBC for five distinct stacking configurations of graphene sandwiched between h-BN layers
  • Phonon transmission and thermal conductance at graphene/Cu interfaces
Significance: These findings are critical for enhancing the performance of graphene- and MoS₂-based electronic devices, where interfacial thermal resistance often dominates the total thermal budget.
DFT Atomistic Green's Function (AGF) Thermal Boundary Conductance MoS₂ / h-BN / Graphene
🧪
Sub-topic C

Phonon Transport Properties of Electronic Materials

A fundamental understanding of thermal transport phenomena in electronic device materials is critical to improving thermal management of high-power-dissipating devices. We use first-principles DFT and the Boltzmann Transport Equation (BTE) to study phonon transport properties including phonon mode contributions to thermal conductivity, three-phonon scattering phase space, and phonon lifetimes.

Recent investigations have covered:

  • 2D Electride Ca₂N: Phonon transport properties of this novel 2D material, exploring its potential for electronic applications
  • β-Ga₂O₃ with vacancies: Effects of oxygen and gallium vacancies on the temperature-dependent thermal conductivity along the [100] direction — critical context for UWBG device design
  • Monolayer MoSe₂: Size-dependence and influence of vacancies and tungsten doping on phonon transport, revealing complex scattering interplay
Impact: These studies provide crucial insights for designing next-generation electronic device materials, particularly for understanding how defects, doping, and vacancy concentrations can be engineered to tune thermal conductivity.
DFT Boltzmann Transport Equation β-Ga₂O₃ MoSe₂ Ca₂N Phonon Scattering

Topic 03

Ultra-Wide Bandgap Materials & Devices
for Power and RF Electronics

Electrothermal modeling, ultrafast thermoreflectance imaging, and phonon BTE simulations to tackle self-heating in β-Ga₂O₃ field-effect transistors — enabling higher breakdown voltages and switching efficiency.

Electrothermal Modeling Thermoreflectance Imaging Phonon BTE Publications

Background: Wide bandgap (WBG) materials such as SiC and GaN offer significant advantages over narrow bandgap materials (Si, GaAs) for power and RF electronics. As WBG technology matures, a new class of ultra-wide bandgap (UWBG) materials with even wider bandgaps is emerging. Gallium oxide (β-Ga₂O₃) is among the most promising candidates — its ultra-wide bandgap enables higher breakdown voltages and switching efficiency than SiC or GaN.

However, the low thermal conductivity of β-Ga₂O₃ leads to severe self-heating in FETs, posing a critical challenge for device viability. Accurate prediction of electrical and thermal characteristics is essential for efficient thermal management and reliable device design.

💻
Sub-topic A

Electrothermal Modeling

We develop 2D electrothermal models for β-Ga₂O₃-based FETs using Synopsys TCAD Sentaurus. These models capture the coupled interplay between electrical transport and heat generation in the device under realistic bias conditions and power dissipation levels.

A key finding is that even when the total power dissipation is identical, different bias conditions (Vds, Vgs combinations) produce dramatically different Joule heat generation profiles within the device — making it critical to model the full coupled electrothermal behavior rather than assuming uniform heat sources.

  • 2D TCAD Sentaurus models for MESFETs and delta-doped β-Ga₂O₃ FETs
  • Study of bias condition effects on Joule heat density spatial distribution
  • Coupling of TCAD with Ansys Fluent and COMSOL for device-level thermal management evaluation
  • 3D thermal modeling procedure in Ansys Fluent for full package simulation
Synopsys TCAD Sentaurus Ansys Fluent COMSOL Coupled Electrothermal Simulation
🔭
Sub-topic B

Ultrafast Thermoreflectance Imaging

We use thermoreflectance imaging to measure spatial and temporal temperature distributions with nanoscale resolution across operating β-Ga₂O₃ devices. The Microsanj NT210B thermoreflectance system with a piezoelectric stage enables high-fidelity transient and steady-state temperature mapping.

This experimental capability allows us to:

  • Measure temperature contours on source, drain, and gate pads at various bias conditions using 100× objective CCD imaging
  • Estimate peak temperatures and their spatial distribution at different power levels
  • Calculate thermal time constants from transient pulse measurements (e.g., 500 µs pulse width, 25% duty cycle)
  • Build and validate compact electrothermal models for transient thermal behavior prediction
Capability: Transient thermal measurements at the gate-center enable direct comparison of measured vs. computed temperatures under different bias and power conditions, providing essential experimental ground-truth for model validation.
Microsanj NT210B Thermoreflectance Imaging Transient Thermal Measurements Thermal Time Constants
⚛️
Sub-topic C

Non-Gray Phonon Transport via Boltzmann Transport Equation

At the device scale, Fourier's law of heat conduction significantly underpredicts hot-spot temperatures in β-Ga₂O₃ FETs because it fails to capture the non-equilibrium phonon transport physics that governs heat dissipation in sub-micron regions.

We solve the non-gray Boltzmann Transport Equation (BTE) for phonon transport in β-Ga₂O₃ thin films and FETs, treating each phonon mode separately to account for mode-dependent mean free paths and scattering rates. This approach:

  • Provides much more accurate temperature distributions near device hot spots compared to Fourier-based approaches
  • Reveals the mechanism of phonon transport within β-Ga₂O₃ FETs at the device level
  • Provides crucial physical insights for future β-Ga₂O₃ device design and thermal management strategies
  • Previously applied to GaN HEMTs, capturing non-gray effects not accessible with Fourier models

Topic 04

Flexible & Printed Electronics

Aerosol jet printing and inkjet printing for low-cost, high-performance CNT thin-film transistors, gas sensors, and hardware security keys — pushing printed electronics toward harsh-environment operation.

Printed CNT FETs Gas Sensors Harsh Environments Publications

Background: The field of printed electronics has gained significant momentum in the last decade. The promise of extremely low-cost electronic components and sensor platforms, enabled by printing technologies such as inkjet printing (IJP) and aerosol jet printing (AJP), is increasingly attainable at large scales.

AJP offers particular versatility: it can process inks with viscosities from 1 to 1000 cP and achieves feature resolutions of 10–20 µm. Available inks include conductors (silver nanoparticles, Ag), semiconductors (carbon nanotubes, CNTs), and dielectrics (hexagonal boron nitride flakes, hBN), enabling full device fabrication in a single additive process.

🖨️
Sub-topic A

Printed CNT Thin-Film Transistors

We fabricate carbon nanotube (CNT) thin-film transistors (TFTs) using aerosol jet printing by carefully controlling the deposition of each functional layer. The transistors use a layer-by-layer additive approach:

  • Electrodes: Silver nanoparticle (Ag NP)-based ink for source, drain, and gate contacts
  • Semiconducting layer: 99.9%-pure CNT-based ink for the active channel
  • Dielectric layer: xdi-dcs ink — a commercially available blend of poly(vinylphenol)/poly(methylsilsesquioxane) — enabling low threshold voltage operation (~5 V)
Key Achievement: By optimizing dielectric layer thickness and composition through thin dielectric layer engineering, we achieved low-voltage CNT TFT operation with threshold voltage ~5 V — critical for integration with portable and battery-powered applications.

We also demonstrate multi-gated CNT FET arrays as physically unclonable functions (PUFs). The inherent variability of CNT deposition creates unique device-to-device signatures that can serve as hardware security keys for IoT and connected device authentication.

Aerosol Jet Printing CNT Thin-Film Transistors Silver Nanoparticles Low-Voltage Operation PUF Security Keys
🌡️
Sub-topic B

Printed CNT Gas Sensors with Identity Verification

Using AJP, we have developed low-cost gas sensors based on CNT thin-film transistors. A fundamental challenge with CNT-based chemical sensors is the lack of selectivity — CNT resistance changes in response to many different gas species, making it difficult to distinguish between them using simple resistance measurements.

To address this, we developed a double exponential-convolution model to fit the sensor's transient response to gas exposure. The key insight is that the two time constants (τ₁, τ₂) extracted from the double-exponential fit are:

  • Unique for each gas type (e.g., NH₃ vs. NO₂ give distinct τ₁/τ₂ pairs)
  • Independent of gas concentration — enabling identity verification regardless of dose
  • Distinct across different TFTs — enabling device-level fingerprinting
Result: The double-exponential time constant approach enables reliable gas identity verification for NH₃ and NO₂ without requiring chemical functionalization or sensor arrays — a significant step toward low-cost, selective printed gas sensors.
CNT Gas Sensors Double Exponential Convolution Model NH₃ / NO₂ Detection Selectivity AJP

Sub-topic C

Harsh-Environment Printed Electronics

Current printed electronics technologies are largely restricted to near-room-temperature operation. This limits their deployment in many high-value applications including industrial process monitoring, engine bay sensors, aerospace components, and downhole oil & gas sensing.

We are actively exploring strategies to extend the operational temperature range of printed electronics:

  • Testing different substrate materials, ink formulations, and device architectures for thermal stability
  • Exploring protective encapsulation strategies for transistors and sensors in elevated-temperature environments
  • Characterizing performance degradation mechanisms at elevated temperatures to guide materials selection
  • Targeting applications where the cost advantages of printed electronics can be realized even in challenging operating conditions
Vision: Extend the temperature performance limits of AJP-printed CNT devices beyond room temperature, opening new application spaces for low-cost electronics in harsh industrial and aerospace environments.
